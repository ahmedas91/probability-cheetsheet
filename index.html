Counting Principles

Counting
Factorial
The number of ways to arrange n distinct objects in a line.
$$n! = n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 1$$
Convention: \(0! = 1\)
Example
How many ways can 5 people line up for a photo?
SOLVE → \(5! = 120\)
\(= 120\)
Counting
Multiplication Principle
If task A has \(m\) outcomes and task B has \(n\) outcomes, doing A then B gives \(m \times n\) outcomes.
$$\text{Total} = n_1 \times n_2 \times \cdots \times n_k$$
Example
A license plate has 3 letters then 4 digits. How many possible plates?
SOLVE → \(26^3 \times 10^4\)
\(= 175{,}760{,}000\)
Counting
Permutations (without repetition)
Choose \(r\) from \(n\), order matters. Filling \(r\) ranked slots.
$$P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n - r)!}$$
Example
A race has 10 runners. How many ways to award Gold, Silver, Bronze?
SOLVE → \(P(10,3) = 10 \times 9 \times 8\)
\(= 720\)
Counting
Permutations (with repetition)
Each of \(r\) slots can be any of \(n\) options — repeats allowed. Think: PINs, passwords.
$$n^r$$
Example
How many 4-digit PINs (digits 0–9)?
SOLVE → \(10^4\)
\(= 10{,}000\)
Counting
Multiset Permutations
Arrange \(n\) objects where some are identical. Divide out redundant orderings.
$$\frac{n!}{n_1!\; n_2!\; \cdots\; n_k!}$$
where \(n_1 + n_2 + \cdots + n_k = n\)
Example
How many distinct arrangements of the letters in MISSISSIPPI?
SOLVE → \(\dfrac{11!}{1!\cdot 4!\cdot 4!\cdot 2!}\)
\(= 34{,}650\)
Counting
Combinations (without repetition)
Choose \(r\) from \(n\), order does NOT matter. Permutations divided by \(r!\).
$$\binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!\,(n - r)!}$$
Example
A committee of 3 is chosen from 8 people. How many ways?
SOLVE → \(\dbinom{8}{3} = \dfrac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{6}\)
\(= 56\)
Counting
Combinations with Repetition (Stars & Bars)
Choose \(r\) from \(n\) types, repeats OK, order doesn't matter. Distributing \(r\) balls into \(n\) bins.
$$\binom{n + r - 1}{r} = \frac{(n + r - 1)!}{r!\,(n - 1)!}$$
Example
A shop has 4 flavors of ice cream. You buy 6 scoops. How many combinations?
SOLVE → \(\dbinom{9}{6} = 84\)
\(= 84\)
Counting
Circular Permutations
Arrange \(n\) distinct objects in a circle. Fix one to break rotational symmetry.
$$(n - 1)!$$
Example
How many ways can 6 people sit around a round table?
SOLVE → \(5! = 120\)
\(= 120\)

Core Probability Rules

Probability
Classical Probability
When all outcomes are equally likely: favorable / total.
$$P(A) = \frac{|A|}{|S|}$$
\(S\) = sample space, \(A\) = event
Example
What is the probability of rolling a sum of 7 with two fair dice?
SOLVE → 6 favorable out of 36
\(= 1/6 \approx 0.167\)
Probability
Complement Rule
Calculate what you don't want and subtract. "At least one" = complement.
$$P(A) = 1 - P(A^c)$$
Example
Roll a die 4 times. What is \(P(\text{at least one 6})\)?
SOLVE → \(1 - (5/6)^4\)
\(= 671/1296 \approx 0.518\)
Probability
Addition Rule (OR)
Add individual probabilities, subtract the double-counted overlap.
$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$
Mutually exclusive: \(P(A \cap B) = 0\)
Example
Draw one card from a standard deck. What is \(P(\text{Heart or King})\)?
SOLVE → \(13/52 + 4/52 - 1/52\)
\(= 4/13 \approx 0.308\)
Probability
Multiplication Rule (AND)
\(P(A \text{ and } B)\): probability of A times probability of B given A happened.
$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A)$$
If independent: just multiply.
Example
Draw 2 cards without replacement. What is \(P(\text{both Aces})\)?
SOLVE → \((4/52)(3/51)\)
\(= 1/221 \approx 0.0045\)
Probability
Independence
Knowing one event tells you nothing about the other.
$$A, B \text{ independent} \iff P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$$
Trap: Independent ≠ mutually exclusive. If both have positive probability and are M.E., they can't be independent.
Example
Flip a fair coin twice. Are "Heads on flip 1" and "Heads on flip 2" independent?
CHECK → \(P(H_1)P(H_2) = 1/4 = P(H_1 \cap H_2)\;\checkmark\)
Yes, independent.
Probability
Inclusion-Exclusion (3 events)
Add singles, subtract pairs, add back triples.
$$P(A \cup B \cup C) = \sum P - \sum P(\text{pairs}) + P(A \cap B \cap C)$$
Example
In 100 students: 40 take Math, 30 take Physics, 20 take CS. 14 take M∩P, 10 take M∩C, 8 take P∩C, 5 take all three. \(P(\text{student takes at least one})\)?
SOLVE → \((40+30+20-14-10-8+5)/100\)
\(= 0.63\)

Conditional Probability & Bayes

Probability
Conditional Probability
Shrink the sample space to only outcomes where B is true.
$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$
Example
Roll two dice. Given the sum is \(\geq 10\), what is the probability both dice show 5 or more?
SOLVE → 4 of 6 qualifying outcomes
\(= 2/3\)
Probability
Law of Total Probability
Break into cases via a partition. Sum over each case weighted by its likelihood.
$$P(A) = \sum_{i} P(A \mid B_i)\, P(B_i)$$
Example
Factory has Machine A (60% of output, 2% defect rate) and Machine B (40%, 5% defects). What is \(P(\text{random item is defective})\)?
SOLVE → \(0.02(0.6) + 0.05(0.4)\)
\(= 0.032\) (3.2%)
★ High-frequency
Bayes' Theorem
Update belief about a hypothesis after evidence. The most important formula for quant interviews.
$$P(B_j \mid A) = \frac{P(A \mid B_j)\, P(B_j)}{\displaystyle\sum_{i} P(A \mid B_i)\, P(B_i)}$$
\(\text{posterior} = \dfrac{\text{likelihood} \times \text{prior}}{\text{evidence}}\)
Classic Interview Example
A disease affects 1% of the population. A test is 95% accurate (both sensitivity and specificity). If you test positive, what's \(P(\text{you have the disease})\)?
TOTAL → \(P(+) = 0.95(0.01) + 0.05(0.99) = 0.059\)
BAYES → \(0.0095 / 0.059\)
\(\approx 0.161\) (16.1%) — base rate dominates

Expected Value & Variance

Probability
Expected Value \(E[X]\)
The long-run average — a probability-weighted sum of all outcomes.
$$E[X] = \sum_{i} x_i \cdot P(X = x_i)$$
$$E[aX + bY] = a\,E[X] + b\,E[Y]$$
Linearity — always holds, even if dependent!
Example
You pay $5 to roll a die. You win $ equal to the face value. What is your expected profit?
SOLVE → \(E[\text{roll}] = 3.5\), profit \(= 3.5 - 5\)
\(= -\$1.50\)
Probability
Variance & Standard Deviation
Measures spread: average squared deviation from the mean.
$$\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - \big(E[X]\big)^2$$
\(\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}\)
$$\text{Var}(aX + b) = a^2\,\text{Var}(X)$$
If independent: \(\text{Var}(X+Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)\)
Example
\(X \sim\) fair die. Find \(\text{Var}(X)\).
SOLVE → \(E[X^2] = 91/6\), so \(91/6 - 3.5^2\)
\(= 35/12 \approx 2.917\)

Joint Distributions, Covariance & Correlation

Fundamental
Joint & Marginal Distributions
A joint distribution describes the probability of two (or more) random variables simultaneously. Marginals recover individual distributions by summing/integrating out the other variable.
$$P(X = x,\, Y = y) = p_{X,Y}(x,y)$$
MARGINAL (DISCRETE)
$$P(X = x) = \sum_{y} P(X=x,\, Y=y)$$
MARGINAL (CONTINUOUS)
$$f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y)\, dy$$
Example
Roll two dice \(X\) and \(Y\). What is \(P(X = 1)\)?
MARGINALIZE → \(\sum_{y=1}^{6} P(X=1,Y=y) = 6 \times \frac{1}{36}\)
\(= 1/6\)
Fundamental
Covariance
Measures how two variables move together. Positive = same direction, negative = opposite, zero = no linear relationship.
$$\text{Cov}(X,Y) = E[XY] - E[X]\,E[Y]$$
Equivalently: \(\text{Cov}(X,Y) = E\big[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)\big]\)
$$\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2\,\text{Cov}(X,Y)$$
\(\text{Cov}(aX, bY) = ab\,\text{Cov}(X,Y)\)
Example
If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent, what is \(\text{Cov}(X,Y)\)?
SOLVE → \(E[XY] = E[X]E[Y]\) when independent
\(\text{Cov} = 0\) (but \(\text{Cov}=0 \not\Rightarrow\) independent!)
Fundamental
Correlation Coefficient
Normalized covariance — always between \(-1\) and \(+1\). Scale-free measure of linear association.
$$\rho_{X,Y} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \,\sigma_Y}$$
\(-1 \leq \rho \leq 1\). \(|\rho| = 1\) iff \(Y = aX + b\) (perfectly linear)
Example
Given \(\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 6\), \(\sigma_X = 3\), \(\sigma_Y = 4\). What is the correlation \(\rho\)?
SOLVE → \(\rho = 6/(3 \times 4)\)
\(= 0.5\)
★ Key technique
Moment Generating Function (MGF)
A transform that encodes all moments of a distribution. If two RVs have the same MGF, they have the same distribution. Derivatives at \(t=0\) give moments.
$$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]$$
\(M_X^{(n)}(0) = E[X^n]\) — the \(n\)-th moment
Key MGFs to know: Bernoulli: \(1-p+pe^t\). Binomial: \((1-p+pe^t)^n\). Poisson: \(e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}\). Normal: \(e^{\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2}\). Exponential: \(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}\) for \(t < \lambda\).
Example
Use the MGF to find \(E[X]\) for \(X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)\).
MGF → \(M(t) = e^{\lambda(e^t-1)}\)
DERIVE → \(M'(t) = \lambda e^t \cdot e^{\lambda(e^t-1)}\)
EVALUATE → \(M'(0) = \lambda \cdot 1 \cdot e^0\)
\(E[X] = \lambda\)

Key Distributions

Fundamental
PMF, PDF & CDF
Three ways to describe a distribution. The PMF/PDF tells you the probability (or density) at each point. The CDF accumulates probability up to a point. Together they fully characterize any random variable.
PMF (DISCRETE)
$$p(x) = P(X = x)$$
\(\sum_x p(x) = 1\)
PDF (CONTINUOUS)
$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$
\(f(x) \geq 0\), \(\int f(x)\,dx = 1\)
CDF (BOTH)
$$F(x) = P(X \leq x)$$
\(f(x) = F'(x)\) for continuous
Key properties of CDF: \(F(-\infty) = 0\), \(F(\infty) = 1\), non-decreasing, right-continuous. And \(P(a < X \leq b) = F(b) - F(a)\).
Distribution
Binomial
# successes in \(n\) independent trials with success prob \(p\).
$$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k}\, p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$
\(E = np \quad \text{Var} = np(1-p)\)
CDF
$$F(k) = \sum_{i=0}^{\lfloor k \rfloor} \binom{n}{i}\, p^i (1-p)^{n-i}$$
Example
Flip a fair coin 10 times. What is \(P(\text{exactly 7 heads})\)?
SOLVE → \(\binom{10}{7}(0.5)^{10} = 120/1024\)
\(\approx 0.117\)
Distribution
Geometric
# trials until 1st success. Memoryless.
$$P(X = k) = (1-p)^{k-1} p$$
\(E = 1/p \quad \text{Var} = (1-p)/p^2\)
CDF
$$F(k) = 1 - (1-p)^{k}$$
Survival: \(P(X > k) = (1-p)^k\)
Example
Roll a die until you get a 6. What is the expected number of rolls? What is \(P(\text{success within 3 tries})\)?
E → \(6\). CDF → \(1 - (5/6)^3\)
\(E=6\); \(F(3) \approx 0.421\)
Distribution
Negative Binomial
# trials until \(r\)-th success. Generalizes Geometric.
$$P(X = k) = \binom{k-1}{r-1} p^r (1-p)^{k-r}$$
\(E = r/p \quad \text{Var} = r(1-p)/p^2\)
CDF
$$F(k) = \sum_{i=r}^{k} \binom{i-1}{r-1} p^r (1-p)^{i-r}$$
Also: \(I_p(r,\, k-r+1)\) via regularized incomplete beta
Example
Flip coins (\(p = 0.5\)) until you get 3 heads. What is \(P(\text{it takes exactly 5 flips})\)?
SOLVE → \(\binom{4}{2}(0.5)^5 = 6/32\)
\(= 3/16 \approx 0.188\)
Distribution
Poisson
Rare events in a fixed interval. Limit of Binomial as \(n \to \infty\), \(p \to 0\), \(np = \lambda\).
$$P(X = k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^k}{k!}$$
\(E = \lambda \quad \text{Var} = \lambda\)
CDF
$$F(k) = e^{-\lambda} \sum_{i=0}^{\lfloor k \rfloor} \frac{\lambda^i}{i!}$$
Example
A server gets 3 requests per minute on average. What is \(P(\text{at most 2 in a given minute})\)?
CDF → \(e^{-3}(1 + 3 + 4.5)\)
\(\approx 0.423\)
Distribution
Hypergeometric
Sampling without replacement from finite population.
$$P(X = k) = \frac{\binom{K}{k}\binom{N-K}{n-k}}{\binom{N}{n}}$$
\(N\) = pop, \(K\) = successes, \(n\) = draws
CDF
$$F(k) = \sum_{i=0}^{k} \frac{\binom{K}{i}\binom{N-K}{n-i}}{\binom{N}{n}}$$
No closed form — direct summation
Example
A deck of 52 cards has 13 hearts. You draw 5 cards. What is \(P(\text{exactly 3 hearts})\)?
SOLVE → \(\binom{13}{3}\binom{39}{2}/\binom{52}{5}\)
\(\approx 0.0815\)
Distribution
Normal (Gaussian)
The bell curve. Arises via CLT. Symmetric around \(\mu\).
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$
\(Z = (X-\mu)/\sigma\) to standardize
CDF
$$\Phi(z) = \frac{1}{2}\!\left[1 + \text{erf}\!\left(\frac{z}{\sqrt{2}}\right)\right]$$
\(\Phi(1) \approx 0.841\), \(\Phi(2) \approx 0.977\), \(\Phi(3) \approx 0.999\)
68-95-99.7: \(\pm1\sigma\) → 68.3%, \(\pm2\sigma\) → 95.4%, \(\pm3\sigma\) → 99.7%.
Example
Heights are distributed as \(N(170, 10^2)\) cm. What is \(P(\text{height} \leq 185)\)?
Z → \(1.5\) → CDF → \(\Phi(1.5)\)
\(\approx 0.933\)
Notation
Standard Normal: \(\Phi(z)\) and \(\varphi(z)\)
Two notations you must know cold for the standard normal \(Z \sim N(0,1)\). Capital \(\Phi\) is the CDF (cumulative area to the left). Lowercase \(\varphi\) is the PDF (the bell curve height). Every normal probability ultimately reduces to these.
UPPERCASE Φ — THE CDF
$$\Phi(z) = P(Z \leq z) = \int_{-\infty}^{z} \varphi(t)\, dt$$
The area under the bell curve to the left of \(z\)
LOWERCASE φ — THE PDF
$$\varphi(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\, e^{-z^2/2}$$
The height of the bell curve at \(z\). Note: \(\varphi(z) = \Phi'(z)\)
KEY IDENTITIES & COMMON LOOKUPS
Symmetry: \(\quad\Phi(-z) = 1 - \Phi(z)\)
Right tail: \(\quad P(Z > z) = 1 - \Phi(z)\)
Interval: \(\quad P(a < Z \leq b) = \Phi(b) - \Phi(a)\)
Symmetric interval: \(\quad P(|Z| \leq z) = 2\Phi(z) - 1\)
General normal: \(\quad P(X \leq x) = \Phi\!\left(\dfrac{x - \mu}{\sigma}\right)\) for \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\)
PDF symmetry: \(\quad\varphi(-z) = \varphi(z)\) (even function)
Key \(\Phi\) values to memorize: \(\Phi(0) = 0.5\), \(\Phi(0.675) \approx 0.75\), \(\Phi(1) \approx 0.8413\), \(\Phi(1.28) \approx 0.90\), \(\Phi(1.645) \approx 0.95\), \(\Phi(1.96) \approx 0.975\), \(\Phi(2) \approx 0.9772\), \(\Phi(2.33) \approx 0.99\), \(\Phi(2.576) \approx 0.995\), \(\Phi(3) \approx 0.9987\).
Example
Let \(X \sim N(50, 8^2)\). Find \(P(34 < X \leq 62)\) using \(\Phi\) notation.
STANDARDIZE → \(Z_1 = \frac{34 - 50}{8} = -2\), \(\quad Z_2 = \frac{62 - 50}{8} = 1.5\)
INTERVAL → \(P(34 < X \leq 62) = \Phi(1.5) - \Phi(-2)\)
SYMMETRY → \(\Phi(-2) = 1 - \Phi(2) = 1 - 0.9772 = 0.0228\)
COMPUTE → \(0.9332 - 0.0228\)
\(= 0.9104\) (91.04%)
Distribution
Uniform (Continuous)
All values in \([a,b]\) equally likely.
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{b-a} \quad x \in [a,b]$$
\(E = (a+b)/2 \quad \text{Var} = (b-a)^2/12\)
CDF
$$F(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x < a \\ \dfrac{x-a}{b-a} & a \leq x \leq b \\ 1 & x > b \end{cases}$$
Example
A bus comes every 15 minutes. You arrive at a random time. What is \(P(\text{wait} \leq 5 \text{ min})\)?
CDF → \((5-0)/(15-0)\)
\(= 1/3 \approx 0.333\)
Distribution
Exponential
Time between Poisson events. Continuous analog of Geometric — memoryless.
$$f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \quad x \geq 0$$
\(E = 1/\lambda \quad \text{Var} = 1/\lambda^2\)
CDF
$$F(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x} \quad x \geq 0$$
Memoryless: \(P(X > s+t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)\)
Example
Calls arrive at rate \(\lambda = 2\) per hour. What is \(P(\text{wait for next call} \leq 30 \text{ min})\)?
CDF → \(1 - e^{-2(0.5)} = 1 - e^{-1}\)
\(\approx 0.632\)

Limit Theorems & Inequalities

★ Essential
Central Limit Theorem (CLT)
The average of many independent RVs is approximately Normal, regardless of the underlying distribution. The reason the Normal is everywhere.
$$\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$
\(\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i\), where \(X_i\) iid with mean \(\mu\), variance \(\sigma^2\)
Example
You roll a fair die 100 times. What is \(P(\text{sample average} > 3.7)\)?
PARAMS → \(\mu = 3.5\), \(\sigma^2 = 35/12\), \(n=100\)
CLT → \(Z = \frac{3.7-3.5}{\sqrt{35/12}/10} = \frac{0.2}{0.1708} \approx 1.17\)
LOOKUP → \(P(Z > 1.17) \approx 1 - 0.879\)
\(\approx 0.121\)
★ Essential
Law of Large Numbers (LLN)
The sample average converges to the true mean as sample size grows. The theoretical basis for Monte Carlo simulation.
$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i \xrightarrow{p} \mu \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$
Weak LLN: convergence in probability. Strong LLN: almost sure convergence.
Example
Flip a fair coin 1,000,000 times. What does the proportion of Heads approach?
LLN → \(\bar{X}_n \to E[X] = 0.5\)
\(\to 0.5\) (with probability 1)
Inequality
Markov's Inequality
The loosest but most general bound. Only requires \(X \geq 0\) — no variance needed.
$$P(X \geq a) \leq \frac{E[X]}{a} \quad \text{for } a > 0,\; X \geq 0$$
Example
\(X \geq 0\) with \(E[X] = 10\). What is an upper bound on \(P(X \geq 50)\)?
MARKOV → \(P(X \geq 50) \leq 10/50\)
\(\leq 0.2\)
Inequality
Chebyshev's Inequality
Tighter than Markov — uses variance. Bounds how far \(X\) can be from its mean in terms of standard deviations.
$$P(|X - \mu| \geq k\sigma) \leq \frac{1}{k^2}$$
Equivalently: \(P(|X - \mu| \geq a) \leq \text{Var}(X)/a^2\)
Example
\(X\) has mean \(\mu = 100\) and standard deviation \(\sigma = 15\). What is an upper bound on \(P(|X - 100| \geq 45)\)?
SOLVE → \(k = 45/15 = 3\) → \(P \leq 1/9\)
\(\leq 1/9 \approx 0.111\)
Inequality
Jensen's Inequality
For a convex function, the function of the mean is ≤ the mean of the function. Appears constantly in finance (pricing, risk).
$$\text{If } g \text{ convex: } \quad g\big(E[X]\big) \leq E\big[g(X)\big]$$
If \(g\) concave: inequality flips. Equality iff \(X\) is constant.
Example
Show \(E[X^2] \geq (E[X])^2\).
JENSEN → \(g(x) = x^2\) is convex, so \(g(E[X]) \leq E[g(X)]\)
\((E[X])^2 \leq E[X^2]\) → This proves \(\text{Var}(X) \geq 0\)!
Inequality
Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality
The correlation coefficient is bounded between \(-1\) and \(1\) — this is why. A workhorse for bounding expectations of products.
$$\big(E[XY]\big)^2 \leq E[X^2] \cdot E[Y^2]$$
Equivalently: \(|\text{Cov}(X,Y)| \leq \sigma_X \sigma_Y\), i.e. \(|\rho| \leq 1\)
Example
Prove \(|\rho_{X,Y}| \leq 1\).
APPLY → \(|\text{Cov}(X,Y)|^2 \leq \text{Var}(X)\text{Var}(Y)\) by C-S
\(|\rho| = |\text{Cov}|/(\sigma_X\sigma_Y) \leq 1\;\checkmark\)

Advanced Concepts & Interview Favorites

★ Classic
Birthday Problem
Pairs grow quadratically, so collisions happen sooner than intuition suggests.
$$P(\text{collision}) = 1 - \prod_{i=0}^{n-1} \frac{365-i}{365}$$
Example
What is the probability that at least 2 of 23 people share a birthday?
COMPLEMENT → \(P(\text{all diff}) \approx 0.4927\)
\(\approx 0.5073\) — just 23!
★ Classic
Derangements
Permutation where nothing is in its original position.
$$D_n = n!\sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{(-1)^k}{k!}$$
\(P \to 1/e \approx 0.368\) as \(n \to \infty\)
Example
4 letters are placed randomly in 4 envelopes. What is \(P(\text{no letter is in the correct envelope})\)?
SOLVE → \(D_4 = 9\), \(P = 9/24\)
\(= 3/8 = 0.375\)
★ High-frequency
Coupon Collector
Draws with replacement to collect all \(n\) types. Sum of Geometric waits.
$$E[T] = n\,H_n = n\!\left(1 + \tfrac{1}{2} + \cdots + \tfrac{1}{n}\right)$$
\(H_n \approx \ln n + 0.5772\)
Example
A cereal box contains one of 6 different toys. How many boxes do you expect to buy to collect all 6?
SOLVE → \(6 \times 2.45\)
\(= 14.7\) boxes
★ High-frequency
Gambler's Ruin
Start \$\(i\), win/lose \$1 per round. Reach \$\(N\) or \$0?
$$p \neq q:\; P_i = \frac{1-(q/p)^i}{1-(q/p)^N} \qquad p = q:\; P_i = \frac{i}{N}$$
Example
Fair coin flips. You start with $4, goal is $10. What is \(P(\text{reaching \$10 before going broke})\)?
SOLVE → \(4/10\)
\(= 0.4\)
Identity / Trick
Indicator Variables
Decompose counts into 0/1 indicators. Use linearity — no independence needed.
$$X = \sum_i \mathbf{1}(A_i) \;\Rightarrow\; E[X] = \sum_i P(A_i)$$
Example
Shuffle a deck of 52 cards. What is the expected number of cards that end up in their original position?
SOLVE → \(52 \times 1/52\)
\(= 1\) (always!)
Identity / Trick
Key Combinatorial Identities
Repeatedly useful when manipulating binomial coefficients.
Symmetry: \(\;\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}\)
Pascal's: \(\;\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}\)
Vandermonde: \(\;\binom{m+n}{r} = \sum_k \binom{m}{k}\binom{n}{r-k}\)
Hockey Stick: \(\;\sum_{i=r}^{n} \binom{i}{r} = \binom{n+1}{r+1}\)
Binomial Thm: \(\;(x+y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^k y^{n-k}\)
Example — Vandermonde
Choose 3 people from a group of 5 men and 7 women. Verify Vandermonde: \(\binom{12}{3} = \sum \binom{5}{k}\binom{7}{3-k}\).
EXPAND → \(35+105+70+10\)
\(= 220 = \binom{12}{3}\;\checkmark\)
★ Key technique
Law of Total Expectation (Tower Property)
Break \(E[X]\) into cases. The go-to for recursive problems.
$$E[X] = \sum_i E[X \mid B_i]\,P(B_i) = E\!\big[E[X \mid Y]\big]$$
Classic Interview Example
What is the expected number of coin flips to get two Heads in a row?
E₀ → \(E_0 = 1 + \frac{1}{2}E_1 + \frac{1}{2}E_0\)
E₁ → \(E_1 = 1 + \frac{1}{2}E_0\)
SOLVE → \(E_0 = 6\)
\(E[\text{HH}] = 6\)
